Everything changed when my mother got sick. Then I was only eleven. The younger ones grew up, and it became much easier for me to look after them. After my mother's illness, all household chores fell on my shoulders. My father earned money to feed us and buy medicine for my mother.
But it doesn't help me.
Mom had cancer. It's so trite, isn't it? She died a year later. I was twelve. Masha is seven, Maxim is six. For all of us, it was a terrible tragedy. My father drank terribly, he was fired due to absenteeism at work. There was practically no money. And we started to starve. I tried to reason with my father, but received only slaps in the face.
For the first time I thought about work after Masha fell into a hungry swoon. It was winter. In a panic, I ran to the neighbors, begging them to pay for cleaning their yard. I didn't want to ask them for food. They already looked at us like garbage.
I chuckled and rolled over on the hard hospital bed. Remembering your life was painful. She was difficult. Especially for a twelve-year-old girl who put the care of her family on her shoulders. Now I realized that I could just ask for food. And most likely, I would not be denied.
But then I cleaned the whole yard and got my money. The shop managed to buy two loaves of bread, milk and even some meat. That evening was one of the most enjoyable of my life. My father was not at home; he was wandering around somewhere with his alcoholic friends. I, having warmed up the milk, poured it into mugs, cut fresh, crispy bread and seated my brother and sister at the table.
From that evening I decided to start earning on my own. Of course, there was also a school. She couldn't be thrown. But I didn't visit often. At first I helped the neighbors, then I was allowed to work in the store for half a day - unloading and arranging products. The owner just felt sorry for us. And it worked for me. They paid a little, of course. But it was enough for me to dress Masha and Maxim. And even a little was enough for food.
The garden also helped out. But the younger ones were already doing it. It was awkward at first, but every year it gets better and better. They both studied well and were obedient. I knew they were trying. I knew they were doing everything they could to make my life easier. And I was extremely grateful to them.
I didn't go to university. I somehow finished school. Not because I was lazy or stupid. I didn't have time to study. I got a job in a small town near our village. My mom taught me how to sew and knit when I was little, so I tried my luck and got a job as a tailor's assistant in town.
The work was difficult. They didn't pay much. But, having heard my story, Aunt Natasha (that was the name of my boss) took me to her apartment. Of course, I greatly embellished my fate by adding a moment with a sick brother and a monster father. Was it bad of me? Definitely. Did I regret it? No.
Living with Aunt Natasha, I did not pay rent and utilities. I only occasionally buy groceries. All the money I saved was sent home. Masha and Maxim were supposed to grow up happy and healthy. After all, it was my duty to look after them.
I worked as a tailor's assistant for three years, after which Aunt Natasha left for another city to live with her children. I stayed on the street. I urgently needed to find a job. And I found her. She started working as a nurse in a hospital. I could not find a cheap apartment or room, so I practically spent the night in the hospital. There was also a shower and beds to sleep in.
